Berlin Visa Requirements —
What Actually Determines Approval

The official requirements list is easy to find. What isn't easy to find is why two professionals with identical documents get different outcomes — and what separates a strong application from one that stalls for months. This is what we've learned from over 500 cases since 2012.

First, the right question

"Visa requirements" is the wrong starting point

Most people searching for "Berlin visa requirements" are actually asking a different question: which visa fits my situation, and what are my realistic chances of approval? The requirements list for each visa type is straightforward. What's less obvious is which category applies to your specific profile — and that single classification decision determines everything that follows.

For skilled professionals moving to Berlin for work, there are two paths that matter. Every other visa type (tourist, student, au pair) is either irrelevant to your situation or not a path to long-term residence.

The Two Paths That Matter

Chancenkarte vs. Skilled Worker Visa

Both lead to Berlin. The difference is where you start.

Chancenkarte — Opportunity Card

No job offer required

A points-based visa that lets you enter Germany and job-search from inside the country. You need at least 6 points across four criteria: qualification level, work experience, language skills, and age.

What most people miss: Your work experience earned anywhere in the world counts — including Gulf countries. A professional with 5+ years in UAE with a recognized degree often qualifies without any German language requirement.

Skilled Worker Visa (Fachkräftevisa)

Job offer required

If you already have a contract from a German employer, this is the faster path. Processing time: 4–8 weeks in most cases. Recognition of your qualification (Anerkennung) may or may not be required depending on your profession.

What most people miss: For IT professionals and many engineering roles, formal recognition is not required — demonstrated experience is sufficient. For healthcare (doctors, nurses), recognition is mandatory before the visa is issued.

Real questions from professionals who've gone through the same process.

Processing time depends on the visa type and embassy. A Skilled Worker Visa typically takes 4–8 weeks after submitting a complete application. The Chancenkarte can take 6–12 weeks. Delays are almost always caused by incomplete documents or qualification assessment started too late — not by embassy backlog.
It depends on the visa type and profession. For the Skilled Worker Visa, B1 German is typically required. For the Chancenkarte, German adds points but is not mandatory if you can score 6 points without it. For healthcare workers (doctors, nurses), B2 German is mandatory before starting work.
The Chancenkarte allows you to enter Germany and search for a job from inside the country — no job offer needed beforehand. A Skilled Worker Visa requires a signed employment contract before you apply. The Chancenkarte is ideal if you are qualified but haven't secured a job yet. The work visa is faster if you already have an employer.
Yes. Once you are in Germany on a valid visa, you apply for a residence permit (Aufenthaltserlaubnis) extension at your local Ausländerbehörde before the visa expires. If you entered on a Chancenkarte and found a job, you switch to a work-based residence permit without leaving Germany.
The visa application fee is €75 for most national visas. Qualification recognition (Anerkennung) costs approximately €100–€300 depending on the authority. Health insurance adds €50–€150/month. Budget €500–€1,000 for the full application process before travel costs.
